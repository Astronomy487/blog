There are so many real numbers, but I care about like 0% of them

Cantor proved that $|\mathbb{R}|>|\mathbb{Z}|$ ($\frak{c}>\aleph_0$) in one of my favorite proofs of all time. The difference between these two infinities can be intuited, I think. But $\frak{c}$ is so monsterously large that you cannot possibly care about something of that size; the set of things that can be conceived of in any capacity is horrifyingly only $\aleph_0$, dare I say finite, even all your favorite irrationals which you usually concede as belonging to the realm of $\frak{c}$.

There are several infinities

I was infatuated with ViHart's video on Cantor's diagonal proof as a child. Anyone with half a brain and half a heart would be, too. It's a surprising, beautiful proof by contradiction that $|\mathbb{N}|<|\mathbb{R}|$, that some infinities are literally bigger than others. It's unintuitive that infinite quantities can be compared like this, but also it totally is intuitive. I feel like I've gotten a sense of how $\mathbb{N}$-like infinities and $\mathbb{R}$-like infinities feel. Does that make any sense?

These infinities can come in lots of names, which I find hilariously idiosyncratic. Really? Lowercase fraktur c ($\mathfrak{c}$)? Which stands for continuum? Here, let me write $\mathfrak{c}$ in my notebook because I can write medieval fraktur letters with my Pilot G2 pen. Get real. Whatever, here's a table.

tiny infinity big infinity
$|\mathbb{N}|$ $|\mathbb{R}|$
$\beth_0$ $\beth_1$
$\aleph_0$ Maybe $\aleph_1$ but we will never be sure
$\mathfrak{c}$

A moment of silence for the symbol $\infty$.

I'm going to call them $\aleph_0$ and $\mathfrak{c}$ here, just because those are the most common names. I'd love to use $\beth_0$ and $\beth_1$ because they look cute next to each other, but that's just hard to read.

Actually, no. What do you want me to call these numbers?

Supersets... not quite

When you first hear about there being more reals than integers, you'll probably quickly point out that this is obvious, since integers are a strict subset of the reals. Everything in $\mathbb{Z}$ is literally in $\mathbb{R}$, but $\mathbb{R}$ has some numbers that aren't in $\mathbb{Z}$, so obviously $\mathbb{R}$ is bigger. But by this same logic, $\mathbb{Z}$ would also be larger than only the even integers $2\mathbb{Z}$, but somehow, it isn't at all. There are as many even integers as integers.

Set theory says that sets have equal size when you make a bijective mapping, and you can easily bijectively map $\mathbb{Z}$ and $2\mathbb{Z}$. But you just can't map $\mathbb{Z}$ to and from $\mathbb{R}$. Cantor said so.

How should you instead intuit the manner in which $\mathbb{R}$ is bigger than $\mathbb{Z}$?

Density... not quite

Maybe you could get this sense that the real numbers are more numerous because they are more dense in the number line. Any interval $[a,b]$ (open or closed, really) (where $a<b$) will have a finite number of integers but an infinite (namely $\mathfrak{c}$) number of reals. This is a very cool property that you could call density, but density does not at all mean a set has cardinality $\mathfrak{c}$! The set of rationals, $\mathbb{Q}$, is definitely dense on the number line but still only has cardinality $\aleph_0$. (I like to think of $|\mathbb{Q}|=|\mathbb{Z}^2|=|\mathbb{Z}|$.)

Then what "makes" $\mathfrak{c}$ so incomprehensibly larger than $\aleph_0$ if not density?

That's a very subjective question, but I like to think about the indescribability of the reals (or of any set size $\mathfrak{c}$).

Oh you're a fan of real numbers? Describe all of them

A great place to start is the fact that the number of possible strings, i.e. the number of possible files, is $\aleph_0$. In my mind, "describable" is logically equivalent to "could be described in a file", so there are $\aleph_0$ number of things you could "describe" while still allowing for all the freedom that the world's many file types grant you. There are more real numbers than that.

What's a computable number? I'm comfortable enough saying "a number computable by an algorithm", albeit within some precision. (Computers only store multiples of powers of two because we live in a messed up world.) Assuming you delegate algorithms to finite description lengths, there are more real numbers than that.

You can't talk about, like, any of the real numbers. Too many of them.

I cannot possibly care about most numbers

The next instinct would be to point out that, in this cardinality $\aleph_0$ set of all files I could imagine, I could only practically store a few of them. I can only store files that fit in my 256 GB drive. For files of length 256 GB, I could store $256^{10^9}$ possible files. For files one byte smaller, I could store $256^{10^9-1}$. I guess, to count all possible file sizes, the number of possible files that will fit on my drive should be $\sum_{L=1}^{10^9}256^L$. Even if I counted wrong, I think you'll appreciate that this number is disgustingly finite. Even for the biggest hard drive imaginable, there are only finite files you could keep.

The set of numbers I could be bothered to talk about is vastly smaller than that. Let's say you have 500 words to impress me, which is, like, 3 KB. There are $256^{3000}$ numbers I care about. I shall call this set $\mathbb{Care}$ because it only has numbers I care about. Wolfram Alpha tells me this cardinality sits at around $2.2\times10^{7654}$.

This is much smaller than $\aleph_0$, and even $\mathfrak{c}$, even though every single irrational number I've ever discussed can be found in $\mathbb{Care}$. We usually think of irrational numbers as belonging to the indescribably huge realm of $\mathfrak{c}$. But for those describable reals, I shall host them in $\mathbb{Care}$.

Is it bad to say I only care about numbers that can fit in 500 words? Maybe one day I'll come across a piece of breathtaking math that just demands a long-winded explanation. I guess some describable things actually just take up that much space, like a big $1000\times1000$ matrix that does something important. It has interesting properties, but no 500-word explanation will actually convey its precise value the way a description can precisely define $\tau$ or whatever. If you're uncomfortable with my bound of 3 kilobytes, just bump that up to something more your style.

Everything you care about is in $\mathbb{Care}$

But look: all of your favorite irrationals, $e$, $\tau$, and your favorite goofy numbers like $i$ and even $\aleph_0$ and $\mathfrak{c}$, and even sets themselves like $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{R}$, and all your favorite vectors and matrices, or even functionsâ€”for anything you could imagine making a set out of, $\mathbb{Care}$ already contains all of those objects which you'll ever reasonably describe. And through it all, $\mathbb{Care}$ is finite.

(Perhaps this is where Russel's paradox strikes. If I'm accepting any kind of mathematical construct in $\mathbb{Care}$, including sets, then $\mathbb{Care}$ must contain itself. We can't have that. I hereby add the condition that your 500-word essay must not be describing $\mathbb{Care}$ itself. Let's be even safer and prohibit sets altogether.)

The Library of Babel is finite. People talk about it like it's infinite, but it isn't. Doesn't that blow your mind?

$|\mathbb{Care}|$ is tiny

Don't get me wrong, $|\mathbb{Care}|=256^{3000}$ feels pretty big. But objectively, it's a tiny number. I can think of way more natural numbers bigger than $256^{3000}$ than natural numbers smaller than $256^{3000}$. I conclude that no real number is big.

Actually, $|\mathbb{Care}|$ must be less than $256^{3000}$. $256^{3000}$ is the number of 3 KB files I could write, but I imagine many of those files will repeatedly refer to the same element in $\mathbb{Care}$. There must be thousands of 500-word love letters to those cool numbers like $1$ or $0$, about which there is so much to say, but also about which only so little needs to be said.

How would you describe your favorite number, given the freedom of 3 kilobytes?
